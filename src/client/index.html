The death of net neutrality in the US and what it means for European internet users

Written by Nick Rutten. Published Januari 24 th, 2017.

A neutral internet

Net neutrality is easiest to explain through a metaphore. Below you can see a highway, going from your computer to the internet.

The highway is the internet. You and everybody else can use it in whatever way they like. The municipality does not give priority to any traffic. Your taxes are being used to maintain the road. This is comparable to a cable company under net neutrality.

Net neutrality means that internet service providers—which you are connected to right now—must treat all data the same. The rules for net neutrality in the US are made by the Federal Communications Commission, or the FCC.

Why does net neutrality matter?

There are very strong arguments in favor of net neutrality.

  • Pseudo Services

    If the internet is not neutral, internet service providers will no longer have reason to invest in their network. Instead, they will invest in useless services to make more money.

    This has already been proven. In the past, several cable companies have charged companies (like Netflix) money so they could avoid having their outgoing network speeds throttled.

  • Digital Rights and Freedoms

    On a neutral internet, freedom of speech is almost guaranteed. Your data is yours, the websites you visit are by choice.

    When the internet would not be neutral, cable companies could block your access to news sites, and instead steer you to their preferred news provider. This allows them to control public opinion in a society that's increasingly dependent on the internet.

  • The end-to-end principle

    According to the end-to-end principle, networks should be dumb. The network itself shouldn't try to manage the data that flows through it, but instead treat all data the same.

    Instead, your phone or computer should take care of handling the data that has been sent to it across the net. Net neutrality supporters feel that the end-to-end is one of the driving principles of the internet today.

  • Keep the web fast!

    Humans are notoriously bad at having patience. We just do not like to wait. If there would not be a neutral internet, your service provider could throttle or block any service, website or transfer at will.

    This would make the experience of using the internet worse for end-users. Because we don't have patience, there might be some content we don't get to see. The creator of that content will never be able to sell his product, if the cable companies doesn't want them too.

  • Innovation

    Allowing a cable company to ask a fee for fast(er) delivery of data would be extortion. New companies and startups might not be able to pay the fee. Only large companies could survive on such an internet. Innovation on the internet would grind to a standstill. We could be stuck with YouTube forever.

  • Control of data

    Supporters of net neutrality want cable companies to be common carriers. That would give any internet service provider the right to use their physical network of cables. Doing so would allow the FCC to enforce net neutrality rules.

    Without this cable companies—who also own television stations and large media internet sites—could potentially be gatekeepers on your internet connection, controlling your access to content or services.

Current situation in the US

On the 14th of December, 2017 a vote was held at the FCC, where the Commission voted to repeal the net neutrality rules that were in effect in the United States.

This vote was announced at November 21, 2017. After the announcement, over 13,000 pages of comments were posted to the FCC‘s website. Between the announcement and the vote, the FCC's leadership appears to have ignored the comments.

Later analysis of the comments showed that thousands of the anti-net neutrality comments had been posted by bots. Most of the pro-net neutrality comments were posted by concerned American citizens. The FCC has done nothing to respond to these comments or to the allegation that a great deal of the comments may be fake.

Courts and senators are now scrambling to stop the repeal of net neutrality rules.

To explain what this might mean for internet consumers in the United States, we can go back to the example of the highway.

The internet today

This is the model of the internet with net neutrality. All traffic is equal and no cars get priority.

The possible future

At the top of this model you see two lanes of traffic. One is blocked. Internet service providers or cable companies can both start blocking internet usage at will. Certain sites, certain services or certain types of data. They can now make you pay more for using the blocked services. Your view of the world might get colored based on what your cable company allows you to see. Remember, these companies own large media corporations.

The red lane is the slow lane. The traffic in that lane is moving slowly because they haven't paid their toll. Cable companies can now charge companies sending you data a fee for delivering the data to you. They are also charging you for not blocking the service!

The green lane is now the “fast lane”. However, that traffic is moving at the same speed it always was. Without net neutrality, cable companies are unlikely to increase their speeds.

What is the impact on European internet users?

So the situation in the US has changed, and the impact on European internet users is potentially large. As a European internet user myself, I recorded all internet traffic on my laptop for around half an hour. During that time, I was working on this article, looking for references on net neutrality, doing research on data visualisation methods, and looking at readme files for several software packages. In other words, I was using the internet. During that time, my computer sent and received over 200,000 packets to destinations around the world. After filtering and aggregating, I found that my computer had sent and received data from 330 unique IP addresses. I was able to find the geographical location of those IP addresses using freegeoip.net.

Geographical distribution of unique IP addresses

The lion's share of my internet traffic goes to the United States. When I recorded the packets, I was in the Netherlands, which is most likely why it comes in a close second. Ireland has a number of datacenters for Google and Amazon AWS, explaining the third place. I find it fairly strange that Austria is higher on the list than Germany. They aren't known for datacenter hosting or great infrastructure. I'm a little concerned about the single IP address in Romania, as it is a well-known hackers paradise.

This means that almost 50% of my internet traffic goes to the United States, more than any other country. Because European internet users are not consumers of American cable companies or internet service providers, we probably won't be blocked or throttled as individuals. However, the services we are using might not be able to afford the ”fast lane” for their data, slowing down our internet usage.

In addition to the short-term effects, the United States has been setting the example for the world for over 50 years. The world looks at the United States and how they solve current issues. Repealing net neutrality might set a dangerous precedent for governments worldwide.

What can we do against it?

In most European countries, net neutrality is protected by law. Changing laws take time. We seem to have the European Union on our side. Net neutrality is a hot topic in politics, and letting your favorite politician know what you want is always a good idea.

If you live in the Netherlands, pay the Bits of Freedom website a visit. They advocate for internet rights. They are a member of EDRi, a European organisation that protects digital freedom. Through them, you can find a movement in your country.

As a final resort, protest!
